# Figure and Visualization Guidelines

## General principle

Figures are central to communicating research. They should be **clear, informative, and self-contained**.

A reader should understand the key message of a figure without needing extensive explanation.

## Key expectations

- figures must be readable at publication size;
- visualizations must support the main claims of the paper;
- style must be consistent across all figures.

## Design recommendations

### Readability

- use sufficiently large fonts;
- ensure good contrast between elements;
- avoid overcrowded visuals.

### Consistency

- use consistent color schemes across figures;
- maintain consistent labeling conventions;
- use the same scale when comparing results.

### Formats

- prefer vector formats (`.pdf`, `.svg`) when possible;
- use high-resolution images for raster formats.

## Specific guidelines for DISC Lab work

### Computer vision outputs

- clearly distinguish predictions vs ground truth;
- use consistent color coding for classes;
- overlay results in a way that preserves interpretability.

### 3D visualizations

- use clean and professional color palettes;
- avoid excessive shading or distracting effects;
- highlight key elements (e.g., damage regions, structures).

### Plots and graphs

- label axes clearly (including units);
- include legends when necessary;
- avoid unnecessary gridlines or decorations.

## Figure captions

- captions should explain what the figure shows;
- include context and key takeaway;
- avoid overly long descriptions.

## Common mistakes

- low-resolution images;
- inconsistent color schemes;
- unclear labels;
- figures that do not support a clear message.

## Final note

Figures are often the most visible part of a paper. Invest time in making them clear and professional.
