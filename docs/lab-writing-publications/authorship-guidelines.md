# Authorship Guidelines

## Principles

Authorship should reflect **substantial intellectual or technical contribution** to the research work.

Authorship is not granted based on position, seniority, or administrative role alone.

## Contribution areas

Typical contributions that may justify authorship include:

- conceptualization of the research problem;
- methodology design;
- data curation and dataset preparation;
- software development and implementation;
- validation and experimentation;
- formal analysis and interpretation of results;
- writing (drafting or significant revision);
- supervision and technical guidance;
- funding acquisition (when coupled with scientific contribution).

## Minimum expectation for co-authorship

A co-author is expected to:

- contribute meaningfully to the scientific content;
- review and approve the manuscript before submission;
- be able to explain and defend the contribution at a reasonable level.

## Authorship order

Authorship order should reflect the level and nature of contribution:

- first author: main contributor to the research and manuscript;
- middle authors: supporting contributors;
- last author: typically the principal investigator (PI), responsible for supervision and research direction.

## Lab-specific expectation

- For work conducted within DISC Lab projects, the PI is expected to be the last author and corresponding author;
- Authorship discussions should happen **early**, especially for multi-student or collaborative projects;
- Changes in authorship must be discussed transparently with all contributors.

## Good practice

- clarify contributions early and revisit when needed;
- avoid adding authors with minimal contribution;
- acknowledge contributors appropriately when authorship is not justified.

Authorship should reflect fairness, transparency, and scientific integrity.
