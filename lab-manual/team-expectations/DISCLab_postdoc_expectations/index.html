    Postdoctoral Researcher Expectations and Working Principles¶

    This document outlines the main expectations, working norms, and professional responsibilities associated with a postdoctoral appointment in the **Digital Intelligence for Structures and Construction research group at NTU Singapore. It is intended to promote transparency, alignment, and a productive working relationship from the outset.

    Purpose of this Document¶

    This document is provided together with the offer of a postdoctoral position to clarify expectations before the appointment begins. Its purpose is to reduce ambiguity, support mutual understanding, and help prevent avoidable misunderstandings at a later stage.

    This document is not an employment contract and does not replace university, school, funding-agency, immigration, human resources, safety, ethics, or other institutional rules. Official NTU policies and the terms of the formal appointment remain controlling where applicable. Instead, this document sets out the research-group-level expectations that the postdoctoral researcher is expected to understand and accept before joining the group.

    Nature of the Appointment¶

    A postdoctoral appointment in the group is a professional research position with a high degree of intellectual responsibility. The position is not limited to executing predefined tasks. It also requires initiative, sound judgement, accountability, and the ability to contribute meaningfully to the scientific direction, delivery, and visibility of the research.

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to:

    • contribute actively to the research objectives of the funded project(s) and the broader mission of the group;
    • work with professionalism, integrity, and collegiality;
    • make steady progress toward high-quality scholarly outputs;
    • help maintain an effective, respectful, and well-organized research environment;
    • operate at a level appropriate for an early-career independent researcher.

    General Professional Expectations¶

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to:

    • behave professionally and respectfully toward all students, staff, collaborators, and administrative personnel;
    • communicate in a timely, clear, and constructive manner;
    • act with honesty in research, reporting, authorship, data handling, and use of resources;
    • respect deadlines, commitments, and agreed responsibilities;
    • raise concerns early rather than allowing issues to accumulate;
    • represent the group and institution professionally in meetings, presentations, conferences, site visits, and online communication;
    • comply with relevant university and laboratory rules, including those related to safety, ethics, confidentiality, and responsible conduct of research.

    Research Performance Expectations¶

    Ownership and initiative¶

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to take ownership of the assigned research agenda. This includes not only carrying out tasks, but also identifying technical bottlenecks, proposing solutions, planning next steps, and helping move the work forward without requiring excessive day-to-day direction.

    The postdoctoral researcher should be able to:

    • understand the scientific goals, technical scope, and deliverables of the project;
    • plan and execute research activities with a reasonable degree of independence;
    • troubleshoot problems systematically and document solutions;
    • critically assess results rather than only generating them;
    • propose new ideas, extensions, and improvements where relevant.

    Quality of work¶

    The group places strong emphasis on rigor and quality. The postdoctoral researcher is expected to produce work that is scientifically sound, technically careful, reproducible where relevant, and presented to a professional academic standard.

    This includes expectations such as:

    • careful experimental design and documentation;
    • appropriate validation, benchmarking, and uncertainty awareness;
    • clean, understandable, and well-documented code and computational workflows;
    • organized data and version control practices;
    • accurate interpretation of results;
    • professional writing for manuscripts, reports, proposals, and presentations.

    Productivity and deliverables¶

    The precise expected outputs depend on the project, contract duration, funding conditions, and stage of the work. However, the postdoctoral researcher is generally expected to contribute meaningfully to several of the following:

    • journal papers and conference papers;
    • technical reports, datasets, codebases, prototypes, or demonstrators;
    • internal project milestones and sponsor deliverables;
    • proposal development for future funding opportunities;
    • mentoring and technical support for students involved in related work;
    • presentations to collaborators, sponsors, or the research community.

    Publication quantity alone is not the only performance measure, but a sustained lack of tangible research output, without justified reasons and prior discussion, will be considered a serious concern.

    Communication, Meetings, and Responsiveness¶

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to maintain regular communication with the supervisor and relevant team members.

    Minimum expectations include:

    • attending scheduled one-to-one meetings, group meetings, project meetings, and other essential research discussions;
    • preparing adequately for meetings, including bringing updates, questions, and next steps;
    • responding to important emails and messages within a reasonable time frame;
    • informing the supervisor in advance when deadlines may not be met;
    • communicating absences, delays, obstacles, or personal constraints that materially affect project progress;
    • sharing information in a timely way rather than working in isolation on issues that affect the team.

    As a general working norm, communication should be proactive. Repeated silence, lack of responsiveness, or lack of visibility over ongoing work is not acceptable in a collaborative research environment.

    Working Style, Presence, and Time Commitment¶

    This is an outcomes-driven academic research position rather than a purely time-clocked role. At the same time, the postdoctoral researcher is expected to demonstrate a professional level of presence, engagement, and reliability.

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to:

    • maintain an appropriate and consistent level of engagement with the project and group;
    • be reasonably available during normal working periods for meetings and collaborative work;
    • spend sufficient time on the research to meet expected progress and deliverables;
    • use flexible working arrangements responsibly, where permitted;
    • discuss in advance any recurring schedule constraints, remote-working plans, travel, or other factors that may affect availability.

    A postdoctoral appointment should not be treated as a passive holding position or as a role with minimal engagement. The level of commitment should reflect the professional nature of the appointment and the expectations associated with funded academic research.

    Reporting and Documentation¶

    Good research management requires proper documentation. The postdoctoral researcher is expected to maintain orderly records of relevant work, which may include:

    • experiment logs and methodological notes;
    • code repositories and version histories;
    • structured storage of datasets, models, scripts, figures, and reports;
    • summaries of results, decisions, and action items;
    • progress updates suitable for internal tracking or sponsor reporting.

    If requested, the postdoctoral researcher should provide concise periodic written updates on progress, obstacles, priorities, and next steps. Research materials generated within the appointment should be stored in a manner accessible to the group and not only on personal devices or private accounts.

    Publications, Authorship, and Academic Credit¶

    Publication culture¶

    The group values high-quality scholarly publication and responsible dissemination. The postdoctoral researcher is expected to contribute actively to manuscript preparation, revision, and submission when publication is an appropriate output of the work.

    Authorship principles¶

    Authorship should reflect substantial intellectual or technical contribution and should follow accepted academic norms as well as any relevant institutional or journal guidance. Authorship order will depend on the nature and extent of contributions and will be discussed in good faith.

    General expectations include:

    • authorship is earned through substantial contribution, not assumed by title or seniority alone;
    • first authorship typically requires a leading contribution to the core research and manuscript preparation;
    • for publications arising primarily from the group's projects, funding, infrastructure, supervision, or intellectual direction, the supervisor must be listed as the last author, serve as the corresponding author, and be acknowledged as the principal investigator;
    • for other publications connected to work conducted within the group, authorship and author order should be discussed with the supervisor at an early stage and determined based on the nature and extent of contributions;
    • all listed authors are expected to contribute, review, and stand by the submitted work to a reasonable extent;
    • acknowledgement may be more appropriate than authorship for limited or peripheral contributions.

    The postdoctoral researcher should not submit manuscripts, abstracts, or related outputs based on group work without prior discussion and approval from the supervisor and relevant co-authors.

    Affiliation and acknowledgement¶

    All outputs arising from the appointment are expected to include the correct institutional affiliation(s), required funding acknowledgements, and other sponsor- or project-specific statements where applicable.

    Data, Code, Intellectual Property, and Confidentiality¶

    Data and code management¶

    Research data, code, models, annotations, figures, notes, and related materials produced as part of the appointment must be handled responsibly and organized professionally. The postdoctoral researcher is expected to follow agreed project conventions for storage, naming, backup, version control, and documentation.

    Ownership and access¶

    Materials generated within the scope of the appointment, especially where tied to funded projects, collaborations, group infrastructure, or institutional resources, should be treated as project or institutional research assets rather than purely personal assets. The postdoctoral researcher must not withhold essential files, repositories, documentation, or access credentials needed for continuity of the research.

    Confidentiality¶

    The postdoctoral researcher may have access to unpublished results, confidential data, sponsor-sensitive information, reviewer discussions, internal proposals, software, or collaboration materials. Such information must not be disclosed, shared externally, or reused inappropriately without authorization.

    Intellectual property and external use¶

    Any potentially patentable, commercially sensitive, or otherwise protected outputs should be discussed before public disclosure. The postdoctoral researcher should not independently commercialize, license, distribute, or externally reuse project materials in ways that conflict with university policy, sponsor terms, collaboration agreements, or the legitimate interests of the group.

    Collaboration and Team Conduct¶

    The group values a constructive, respectful, and collaborative culture. The postdoctoral researcher is expected to contribute positively to that environment.

    This includes:

    • interacting respectfully with students and colleagues;
    • giving technically sound and professional feedback;
    • avoiding dismissive, hostile, or inappropriate behavior;
    • recognizing that collaboration requires transparency and mutual accountability;
    • supporting a culture in which junior members can ask questions and seek guidance without fear of disrespect.

    Where the postdoctoral researcher works with students, interns, research assistants, or visitors, they are expected to act as a responsible senior member of the team and model good academic and professional conduct.

    Mentoring and Support of Junior Researchers¶

    Depending on project needs, the postdoctoral researcher may be asked to support the technical mentoring of PhD students, master students, undergraduate students, visiting students, or research staff. This may include onboarding, providing technical guidance, reviewing code or manuscripts, helping design experiments, or advising on good research practice.

    Such mentoring is part of the professional role when relevant and should be done responsibly. However, the postdoctoral researcher should also maintain appropriate boundaries and communicate clearly when supervisory responsibility remains with the faculty supervisor.

    Funding, Resources, Travel, and Expenses¶

    Access to equipment, software, computing resources, travel, consumables, or project-related expenses is subject to budget availability, institutional rules, project scope, and supervisory approval.

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to:

    • use research funds and resources responsibly;
    • seek approval before incurring reimbursable expenses where required;
    • align conference and travel plans with project priorities and available resources;
    • understand that not all conferences, travel requests, or purchases can necessarily be supported;
    • handle institutional assets with care.

    Compliance, Ethics, and Safety¶

    The postdoctoral researcher is expected to comply with all applicable requirements related to research integrity, human subjects, data governance, safety training, laboratory or workshop rules, fieldwork procedures, and any project-specific ethical or regulatory approvals.

    No research activity requiring prior approval should begin before the relevant approvals are in place.

    Communication of Concerns and Resolution of Issues¶

    A productive research environment requires open, respectful, and timely communication in both directions. The postdoctoral researcher is encouraged to raise concerns early whenever a problem, misunderstanding, or source of friction arises that may affect the research, working relationships, or the overall working environment.

    Examples of concerns that should be communicated early include, but are not limited to:

    • misunderstandings regarding responsibilities, expectations, or priorities;
    • difficulties in communication or collaboration with other group members, students, staff, or external collaborators;
    • concerns about authorship, acknowledgement, contribution, or publication planning;
    • disagreement regarding research direction, technical decisions, or allocation of effort;
    • obstacles affecting progress, including lack of clarity, resource limitations, or personal circumstances that materially affect the work;
    • any situation in which an issue may worsen if left unaddressed.

    In most cases, concerns should first be raised with the supervisor in a professional and constructive manner. The purpose of such discussion is to clarify expectations, understand the issue, and identify a reasonable path forward. Where appropriate, concerns involving other members of the group or collaborators may also be addressed through a joint discussion with the relevant parties.

    Concerns raised in good faith will be treated seriously and respectfully. Open communication is considered an important part of maintaining a healthy, fair, and productive research environment.

    Performance Concerns and Escalation¶

    If concerns arise regarding performance, conduct, communication, professionalism, or fulfilment of responsibilities, the usual approach will be to discuss them directly and in good faith at an early stage. Depending on the issue, expectations may be clarified verbally or in writing, and reasonable time may be given for improvement where appropriate.

    Examples of issues that may require attention include, but are not limited to:

    • persistent lack of progress without reasonable explanation;
    • repeated failure to meet agreed responsibilities or deadlines;
    • poor communication or prolonged unresponsiveness;
    • failure to document or share essential research materials appropriately;
    • serious lapses in professional conduct or research integrity;
    • behaviour that negatively affects the working environment or project continuity.

    Nothing in this document limits the application of formal institutional procedures where required.

    End-of-Appointment Responsibilities¶

    Before the appointment ends, the postdoctoral researcher is expected to support an orderly transition. This includes, as applicable:

    • organizing and transferring project files, repositories, data, models, and documentation;
    • summarizing the status of ongoing tasks, manuscripts, and deliverables;
    • returning equipment, access items, or other institutional property as required;
    • clarifying any remaining publication, authorship, or handover matters.

    Departure should not leave the project in a state where critical knowledge or materials are inaccessible.

    Mutual Understanding¶

    The goal of this document is not to create rigidity, but to set a professional foundation for an effective working relationship. The supervisor is committed to providing academic guidance, regular feedback, reasonable access to discussion, and support for the postdoctoral researcher’s professional growth. In return, the postdoctoral researcher is expected to engage seriously with the role and to uphold the standards outlined above.
